Welcome & Session Overview

Welcome to a space of kindness, understanding, and growth.

In this session, we'll explore how our inner voice shapes our experience and learn practical ways to cultivate a more compassionate relationship with ourselves.

This is a safe, non-judgmental space. You're invited to participate at your comfort level and take care of your needs throughout our time together.

Self-compassion is a skill that can be learned and developed with practice. Today is just the beginning of what can be a transformative journey.

"With self-compassion, we give ourselves the same kindness and care we'd give to a good friend."
— Kristin Neff

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

Understand the difference between self-criticism and self-compassion

Identify the three core components of self-compassion

Practice specific techniques for developing self-compassion

Create a personal plan for integrating self-compassion into daily life

Understanding Self-Criticism

Self-criticism is the harsh, judgmental inner voice that:

Focuses on perceived failures and shortcomings

Uses language like "I'm stupid," "I always mess up," or "I'm not good enough"

Often leads to feelings of shame, inadequacy, and isolation

Can become a habitual pattern that feels automatic and unchangeable

"The way we talk to ourselves matters. It shapes our reality and our capacity for growth."

What is Self-Compassion?

"Self-compassion involves treating yourself with the same kindness and understanding you would offer a good friend during difficult times."
— Dr. Kristin Neff

It's not self-pity, self-indulgence, or making excuses

It's a balanced approach to negative emotions and experiences

It creates space for growth and learning rather than harsh judgment

It provides emotional resilience and psychological safety

Where Does Self-Criticism Come From?

Early Experiences

Critical parenting or caregiving

Academic or social pressures

Trauma or difficult life events

Cultural Influences

Achievement-oriented culture

Social comparison and perfectionism

Protective mechanism against future hurt

"Understanding the origins of our inner critic helps us respond with compassion rather than judgment."

The Three Elements of Self-Compassion

Self-Kindness

Common Humanity

Mindfulness

"These three elements work together to create a foundation of emotional well-being and resilience."

Self-Kindness

Instead of Self-Criticism

"I'm such an idiot"

"I always mess everything up"

"I should be better than this"

Practice Self-Kindness

"I made a mistake, and that's human"

"This is a difficult moment for me"

"May I be kind to myself right now"

"Self-kindness means treating yourself with the same warmth and understanding you'd offer a dear friend."

Common Humanity

Recognizing that suffering and imperfection are part of the shared human experience

Isolation Thinking

"I'm the only one who struggles like this"

"Everyone else has it figured out"

"Something must be wrong with me"

Common Humanity

"This is part of being human"

"Others have felt this way too"

"I'm not alone in this experience"

"Suffering is not a personal failing—it's a universal human experience that connects us all."

Mindfulness

Observing our thoughts and feelings with awareness and without judgment

Over-Identification

Getting swept away by emotions

Believing every thought we have

Ruminating and catastrophizing

Mindful Awareness

Noticing thoughts and feelings as they arise

Observing without immediately reacting

Creating space between self and experience

"Mindfulness allows us to hold our pain in loving awareness, neither suppressing nor being overwhelmed by it."

Practice: Self-Compassion Break

When you notice you're struggling, try this 3-step practice:

Step 1: Mindfulness
"This is a moment of suffering" or "This is difficult right now"

Step 2: Common Humanity
"Suffering is part of life" or "I'm not alone in feeling this way"

Step 3: Self-Kindness
"May I be kind to myself" or "May I give myself the compassion I need"

"This simple practice can be done anywhere, anytime you need a moment of self-compassion."

Practice: Writing a Compassionate Letter

Write yourself a letter from the perspective of a loving, wise friend:

Writing Prompts:

"I understand that you're going through a difficult time..."
"It's completely normal to feel this way when..."
"What you need to hear right now is..."
"Remember that you are worthy of love and kindness because..."

Tip: Write as if you're comforting your best friend who came to you with this same struggle.

Throughout the Day

Self-Compassion in Writing

Transform your inner dialogue through compassionate writing:

Critical Voice

"I can't believe I made that mistake. I'm so stupid. I'll never get this right."

Compassionate Voice

"I made a mistake, and that's part of learning. Everyone makes mistakes. What can I learn from this experience?"

Practice Exercise

Write down a recent self-critical thought, then rewrite it with compassion. Notice how the reframe feels different in your body.

Key Takeaways

Self-compassion is learnable: It's a skill that develops with practice, not a fixed trait

Three components work together: Self-kindness, common humanity, and mindfulness

It builds resilience: Self-compassion helps us bounce back from setbacks more effectively

Start small: Even brief moments of self-compassion can make a meaningful difference

"The relationship you have with yourself sets the tone for every other relationship in your life."
